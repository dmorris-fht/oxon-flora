### *Pulsatilla vulgaris* Pasqueflower 
Mill.
#### Ecology
This spectacular perennial is confined to calcareous pasture and has suffered a big decline. It prefers steep south and southwest facing slopes with low-nutrient soils species-rich sward. It is vulnerable to grazing, especially the flowers, but it requires short turf.
#### Status & distribution
In central and southern England as far north as Leeds. Formerly on the Wessex Downs, Cotswolds and Chilterns, recently only native on the Wessex Downs.
#### Trends
Druce (1927) listed four sites and Bowen (1968) noted six in vc22 Oxon., but Killick et al. (1998) reported it as lost in the 1880s from vc23. During 2002-22 only native at Aston Upthorpe SSSI.
#### Conservaton work
Part of the last, small, population at Aston Upthorpe was rabbit fenced in the 1970s, but this led to the grass becoming very dense with a reduction in survival and flowering of the enclosed Pasqueflowers. As it is believed rarely to establish naturally from seed, the small population at Aston Upthorpe was reinforced in 1999 with c. 90 plants raised from seed collected from the same site. Establishment was initially high and the exclosure fencing was opened to allow sheep grazing later in the year. In 2012 51 plants survived from the reinforcement and 12 natural plants were present. Despite the rabbit-netting a significant number of flowers are grazed off before S E Erskinedset unless given additional protection. A new population was introduced at Hartslock BBOWT NR in 1998 using plants from S E Erskined from Bedfordshire. Seventy-nine plants survived in 2012 with naturally arising seedlings making up for initial losses among the original transplants and thus producing a small population increase despite many of the flowers being grazed off. Stock from Hertfordshire was introduced to Warburg Reserve both at Leahill Wildlife Garden (8 in 2010) and at Maidensgrove Field (5 in 2006).
